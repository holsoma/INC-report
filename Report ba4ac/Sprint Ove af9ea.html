Sprint Overview

What is Scrum?

Scrum is a lightweight framework that comes in handy for individuals, teams, and organizations to improve deliverables by generating value through adaptive solutions to complex problems. Sounds easy, but is extremely difficult to implement and use effectively.

The team went through 5 sprints over the course of the semester, with each sprint becoming increasingly efficient and effective. By the end of the 5 sprints, the group summarised AGILE methodology and Scrum into 3 words. Feedback, Changes, Adaptability. It was due to the regular feedback from the clients, that molded our changes, as well as the teams adaptability to allow the development of Red Team’s AI-enabled query system to come into fruition.

SPRINT 1️⃣

In the first sprint the group went through what we’ll call an “exploration” phase. We had just inerited an AI-query system from the SGUS team. As such the team was able to explore what the previous group has done. We also went through a series of stress tests, to figure out the plausible issues with the original model, which led to issues such as duplicate answers, issues with the provided Comma-Seperated-Value(CSV) file, used to train the model.

Following which we had a meeting with the client, AV Intelligence where they shared their views and concern. The original pain point they had was “Requiring a tool to aid in HR onboarding/employee onboarding.” When we asked how they envisioned their problem to be solved, AV intelligence replied with “Solving a day to day problem as well as aid with employee onboarding.” This meeting led to creation of some user stories such as “being able to add new documents with ease, to maintain the solutions longevity” as well as “an easy to use interface, so that the solution is easily accessible”

With this, we came to the end of the sprint. This being our first sprint, meant that many of the scrum processes were not done perfectly, but our efficiency in Scrum improved over the course of the next few sprints. With every ending, there is a new beginning, for us, this was Sprint 2.

SPRINT 2️⃣

In Sprint 2, the team embarked on an “Design and Development” phase. For us design meant exploring the different models available, comparing one against another, and weighing the pros and cons of it. After much research, the team discovered, haystack.

Haystack is an open source NLP framework that leverages transformer models from the hugging-face model hub with its extensive documentation, it meant that haystack’s impressive capability can be harnessed by the team. It was also good for Closed Domain Question Answering (CDQA)

With the intention to continue using Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers (BERT) based models due to its reputation as one of the biggest path-breaking developments in the field of Natural Language Processing (NLP) as well as it being used by Google itself, we placed our confidence in BERT.

This meant that exploration on the intricacies of BERT and how it gels together with haystack was in order.

Naturally, with developments in the model side, the team had to come up with a front-end to show case the models capabilities. The team decided on React with Firebase. We chose this due to Firebase’s extensive capabilities and available SDKs, which we thought was able to make the completions of our user stories more easily.

Coming to the end of the sprint, the team was in for a shock, as we were informed of the possibility of needing to use a different method, namely the golden retriever. During our Sprint Review, we ran a docker demo to look at the source code locally and assessed the completeness of code as well as scalability. We were able to use the model to generate answers to the clients queries, leaving them optimistic about our models capabilities and potential.

Our final iteration for the model in this sprint utilized Haystack, alongside ElasticSearch document store, due to its speed and accuracy as well as wide usage. The team also had to decide between 2 different retrieval methods, namely Sparse Retrieval and Dense Retrieval. After much deliberation, the team chose the Dense Retrieval method, due to it being more powerful, requiring a small training corpus.

With this Sprint 2 came to an end.

Sprint 2 went much smoother in terms of scrum processes, due to the team having collated prior experience from the previous sprint, the team was able to go through the various scrum meetings with ease, and each meeting adhered well to the agenda and in a timely fashion.

SPRINT 3️⃣

Sprint 3 was a rocky sprint. Numerous issues sprang up, causing massive hindrance to the teams development.

Firstly, the Golden Retriever. Developed by AI Singapore (AISG), is an open source, automated information retrieval engine for human language queries. Utilizing the Universal Sentence Encoder for Question and Answering (Google USE-QA) to power itself, the Golden Retriever takes in CSVs as datasets to work. This, amongst other reasons, steered us away from this solution. By requiring CSVs, it meant that the user had to manually create the datasets, which did not solve a big part of our original user requirement. (Being able to upload PDF documents to train the model).

Secondly, due to our original implementation of Firebase, it caused issues with the document stores used for the model. As such we had to pivot from the original idea of using Firebase with react, to look into alternatives such as Flask, and Streamlit.

In this sprint we also carried out our first sprint retrospective, due to the rockiness of the sprint. With the intention of better carrying out scrum processes for the remaining sprints. The sprint retrospective allowed for the team to perform with better cohesion and solution to problems. This concluded sprint 3.

SPRINT 4️⃣

Sprint 4, was the last full sprint left for the team. In this sprint the project was nearing the end as such the team entered a “optimization” phase, where we looked to further improve the model.

Firstly, we explored spaCy an open-source software library for advanced natural language processing to extract information from text and perform Named Entity Recognition(NER) and part-of-speech (POS) tagging, in conjunction with PreProcessor, which is an in-built class defined in the haystack library. PreProcessor allows for the normalization of white space, the cleaning of header and footers, and splitting by paragraphs, in an attempt to optimize query performance. Ultimately, we decided on PreProcessor, due to its convenience as it was already in built to haystack, as well as its ability to better tackle our use case, due to haystack’s semantic search capability.

Secondly, we researched on ways to automate model retraining. We approached the active haystack community for guidance and found that retraining the model back on answers did not have significant benefits, and would only be useful if wrong answers were filtered out and manually replaced with correct ones, meaning this would be a human-in-the-loop approach. As this feature was only briefly touched upon in a blog post by deepset, the team decided against the semi-automated approach as there was no full-support for it.

Thirdly, we implemented a ranker, which is an extra node which ranks the answers returned by the retriever. This was a unique take on the implementation of ranker as it was traditionally used with Sparse retrieval methods, despite this, we found the implementation of a ranker to improve our answer accuracy. As such we decided to use the SentenceTransformersRanker to boost our models performance.

In terms of sprint processes, there were major improvements from Sprint 3. Members found it easier to attend meetings, due to the meetings being pushed back, and were generally more participative. Members were also less afraid to clear doubts, and managed the scrum processes well.

This concluded Sprint 4.

SPRINT 5️⃣

Sprint 5, the final sprint. In this sprint, the team looked to tie together the various components, into a homogeneous system. In order to piece together our final model, we have to first understand the haystack pipelines. The haystack pipelines chain together various haystack components to create a search system, utilizing the pipeline class. The primary concept behind a pipeline class is to build a directed acyclic graph (DAG) where each node is a building block, which will all come together to form the search system.

For us, we decided to take an innovative route to building our pipeline. Instead of creating a pipeline from the top, we dissected the ready made ExtractiveQAPipeline, and added our own custom nodes to better fit our requirements. This allowed us to create a pipeline that compiles, build and deploys our code to be utilized in the QA system in an efficient manner.

With our model achieving satisfactory results and our production of an incremental PSP, we utilized the various REST API endpoints to integrate additional functionalities to help solve our user stories.

We implemented a file upload page, where users can upload and delete files with ease. This allows the company to remove outdated policies for newer ones with ease, which is a key requirement identified.

With the addition of a History page, users can then see past queries as well as the respective answer returned, allowing for better accountability and reliability.

We also added a feedback page, whereby users with higher privileges, (i.e ROOT and Master) to view the queries and feedback from different users, to understand how the model is faring.

The team utilised hydralit, a wrapping and template project to combine multi-page application on streamlit. This made it easier to create a user-friendly UI, improving user experience when utilising our AI-enabled query page due to its convience when navigating from page to page.

This marked the end of the model building and our final product was done. But with any good project, the necessary documentations such as user guides, developer guides etc. needed to be done. As such the team split up the work, utilizing the strengths of each team player to smoothly finish the documentation.

This also marked the end of sprint 5, and the long arduous journey in developing this AI-enabled query system. Throughout the project, the team not only learned more about NLP, but also extremely vital soft skills, such as teamwork and good communication skills. The team also became extremely well versed in the various scrum processes, and is now confident in utilizing them to its full capabilities in future projects.
